What is Adani and the Carmichael Mine?

Adani is a multinational conglomerate that is currently preparing to build the Carmichael Mine: Australia's largest coal mine, 550km south of Cairns in Queensland's Galilee Basin.


12 Reasons to #StopAdani | PDF

The largest Mine

Once operating, the Carmichael mine will be the largest in the Southern Hemisphere and 3rd largest in the world by capacity, exporting 60 million tonnes of low-quality, high-ash thermal coal to the Indian market each year.

In the process it will emit over 86 million tons of CO2 into the Australian atmosphere and 4.73 billion tonnes of CO2 worldwide; effectively offsetting 97.5% of Australia's 2030 greenhouse gas reduction targets. To put it in perspective, if the greenhouse gas emissions resulting from the burning of Galilee Basin coal were compared to those from other countries, the Galilee Basin would rank as the world’s seventh largest contributor of CO2 pollution.

When we ramp up the mine, everything will be autonomous from mine to port.

Jeyakumar "JJ" Janakaraj
CEO Adani Mining

Coal Mine Realities

Australian and International Scientists writing in the journal Nature have stated unequivocally that "immediate global action to curb future warming is essential to secure a future for coral reefs". The opinions are strikingly similar to those professed by both Barack Obama and David Attenborough during their reknowned 2015 White House interview where they spoke specifically of the threat to the Great Barrier Reef from climate change and the need for a global response.

While a small number of commentators insist the Adani mine will be a boon for Cairns and the region, spruiking the prospect of jobs and infrastructure to a seemingly desperate populace, most Australians (up to 93% in some polls) are opposed to many of the realities of the mine.

Controversially Adani is set to receive a lucrative deal on mining royalty payments, drastically reducing the initial dollar amount they pay per ton to extract and sell our mineral resources. On top of this, the company is demanding a loan of up to $900 million for construction of a railway line, the balance of which Guardian Australia has reported will be directed through Adani-linked offshore shell companies, making due dilligence impossible.

Nor does Adani’s history bode well for the region. The corporation has a tragic record of human rights and environmental abuses, accused of massive worker exploitation including child labour, state-level bribery, ongoing global pollution disasters, and most recently reports of a massive contamination at Abbot Point, south of Townsville. Many of those involved in Adani disasters in Zambia and India now hold key positions in the Australian operation. The ABC has also reported on the Adani Group's complex and secretive internal structure, with a number of subsidiaries registered in the Cayman Islands, Mauritius, the UAE and Singapore (all known tax havens), raising serious concerns over profit-shifting and liability.

Land of the 'Fair Go'

There is also currently a battle in the Federal Court over land rights, with the Wangan and Jagalingou people, Traditional Owners of the 462km2 tract of the Galilee Basin the mine is to be built upon, firmly rejecting the agreement in place with Adani. Lawyers for the group continue to fight extraordinary intervention from prominent Government figures on Adani's behalf, including a promise from Prime Minister Turnbull to 'fix' Native Title for Adani. Previous attempts to block the development through the courts have proven unsuccessful.

While Traditional Owners from across the country sit on both sides of the debate, many have spoken out against the mine, including in Cairns where protests have seen representatives from Gimuy walubarra yidinji group, Yirriganydji, Mamu, Djabugai, Kuku Yalanji and Juru making their concerns clear. Speaking to a crowd of over 700 people, Nerelle Nicol, a Juru woman whose traditional land includes Abbot Point, voiced her frustrations at the shortsightedness of the proposed mine, the negative impact it would have on the country and future generations, and the fact she thought governments should be embracing renewable energy sources.

Farmers have felt similar frustrations. Adani was recently awarded unlimited access to groundwater for the life of the mine, along with similar access to surface water, from which it plans to draw upwards of 4.7 gigalitres per year. Both licenses were handed over with little scrutiny, and are exempt from future water management initiatives. Local grazier Bruce Currie, who has joined the fight through the courts, describes the decision as 'barbaric'.

This mine will affect our reef and our land, so it will effect our songlines and our story sites.

Nerelle Nicol, Juru woman & Traditional Owner
Stop Adani Rally, 18 June 2017

Employment Priorities

Nearly 70,000 jobs rely on the health of the Great Barrier Reef. Nonetheless 'jobs' is also the main argument coming from proponents of the mine. Despite vastly exaggerated claims, official figures presented to the QLD Land Court by Adani confirm 1,200 net jobs would be created in the region over the life of the mine, with another 264 in capital cities. These numbers are in sharp contrast with the company's 'estimated' figures of 10,000 regional jobs (one misleadingly echoed by both state and federal governments). Adani's contempt for regional workers has been further highlighted by statements to Business Review Australia asserting that the mining operation would be largely unmanned: "All the vehicles will be capable of automation. When we ramp up the mine, everything will be autonomous from mine to port."

this is going to definitely impact on the integrity of [the Great Artesian Basin].

Bruce Currie
Grazier, Central QLD
